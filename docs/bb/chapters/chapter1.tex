\section{Background and Motivation}
\justify
The integration of mobile computing, artificial intelligence (AI), and conventional
medicine signifies a pivotal frontier in contemporary digital health. The World Health
Organization (WHO) says that 88\% of all countries use traditional and complementary
medicine (T\&CM), with more than 170 member states reporting the use of herbal medicines,
acupuncture, yoga, indigenous therapies, and other types of traditional medicine~[1]. Ayurveda,
or ``Science of Life,'' has been practiced in India for more than 3,000 years. It takes a
holistic view of health and divides human physiology into three bio-energetic forces, or
\textit{Doshas}: \textit{Vata} (Kinetic Energy), \textit{Pitta} (Transformative Energy),
and \textit{Kapha} (Cohesive Energy)~[10].

\textit{Dravyaguna Vigyan}, the study of the properties of medicinal substances, is the most
important part of Ayurvedic pharmacology. Ayurveda classifies plants based on their
\textit{Rasa} (Taste), \textit{Guna} (Quality), \textit{Virya} (Potency), \textit{Vipaka}
(Post-digestive effect), and \textit{Prabhava} (Specific action), while Western pharmacology
looks for active chemical compounds like alkaloids and glycosides~[9].
\textit{Ocimum sanctum} (Tulsi) is not only an antibacterial agent; it is categorized as
possessing a \textit{Katu} (Pungent) \textit{Rasa} and an \textit{Ushna} (Heating)
\textit{Virya}, rendering it suitable for balancing \textit{Kapha} and \textit{Vata}
disorders while potentially exacerbating high \textit{Pitta} conditions~[9].

Even though Ayurveda is very deep, it is going through a crisis in the 21st century.
The ability to identify medicinal plants, which was once passed down through oral traditions
(\textit{Gurukula}), is fading. Urbanization has separated most people from their natural
surroundings. Because there are not enough qualified \textit{Vaidyas} (Ayurvedic doctors),
millions of people cannot get personalized diagnosis. This creates an urgent need for a
``Digital Vaidya''---a system that uses mobile technology to make expert knowledge
accessible to everyone.

\section{Relevance}
\justify
AyurSpace is highly relevant in today's healthcare and technology landscape because it
directly addresses the widening gap between the depth of Ayurvedic knowledge and its
practical accessibility to the general population. India alone has over 600 million
smartphone users, yet there exists no intelligent, AI-powered mobile application that
bridges traditional plant medicine with modern diagnostic reasoning. The 88\% of WHO
member states that rely on traditional and complementary medicine~[1] represent a global
user base with no reliable digital tool to identify medicinal plants and receive safe,
personalized Ayurvedic guidance.

The relevance of AyurSpace is further underscored by the rapid erosion of oral
\textit{Gurukula} traditions. With fewer qualified \textit{Vaidyas} available in rural
and semi-urban areas, millions of people self-medicate using plants they cannot accurately
identify. Misidentification of medicinal herbs---such as confusing \textit{Bacopa monnieri}
with \textit{Centella asiatica}, both commonly called ``Brahmi''---can lead to incorrect
dosage and adverse health effects. AyurSpace resolves this critical safety issue by
combining computer vision-based species identification with Gemini LLM-powered Ayurvedic
reasoning, ensuring that plant identification always precedes any health recommendation.

Furthermore, in an era where telemedicine and digital health are becoming mainstream,
the ability to deliver personalized \textit{Prakriti} (constitution) assessment and
AI-driven wellness guidance through an affordable mobile application is transformative.
AyurSpace empowers individuals in underserved regions---where access to Ayurvedic
practitioners is scarce---with the same quality of guidance previously available only
in specialized clinics. This cross-platform Flutter implementation ensures reach across
both Android and iOS devices without additional infrastructure cost, making expert
Ayurvedic knowledge accessible, accurate, and safe for everyone.
